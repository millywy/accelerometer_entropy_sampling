Background and Motivation
The core scientific question is: Is accelerometer entropy/compressibility a better predictor of PPG-based heart rate estimation error than accelerometer magnitude?
Current wearable devices (like Apple Watch) use accelerometer (ACC) magnitude to decide PPG signal quality — high magnitude means lots of motion, so they either turn off HR estimation or increase sampling rate. The problem is that magnitude only tells you how big the motion is, not how predictable it is.
Our hypothesis is that compressibility (measured via entropy) is more informative than magnitude. The intuition is:

Treadmill running: High ACC magnitude BUT highly periodic/predictable motion → high compressibility → HR estimation should still work well
Arm movements (boxing, hand gestures): Low ACC magnitude BUT highly irregular/unpredictable motion → low compressibility → HR estimation breaks down

These two cases are where magnitude and entropy disagree — and we want to show entropy is right and magnitude is wrong.

Step 2 — Simple Stateless HR Estimator
This is critical: the estimator must be completely stateless — no history, no tracking across windows, no dependence on previous estimates. Each 8-second window is processed independently.
Algorithm:

Average the two PPG channels
Bandpass filter between 0.5–4 Hz (covers 30–240 BPM) using a Butterworth filter
Compute FFT of the filtered window
Find the dominant frequency peak within the 0.5–4 Hz range
Convert to BPM

Windowing: 8 seconds = 1000 samples at 125 Hz, shift by 250 samples (2 seconds) between windows, matching the BPMtrace ground truth exactly.
Validation:

Compare estimated BPM to BPM0 ground truth for all windows
Compute per-window absolute error: |estimated - ground_truth|

Step 4 — Entropy Metrics
Compute the following metrics for the ACC signal in each 8-second window. Use the combined 3-axis ACC signal (you can try per-axis and combined and see which correlates better):
Metric 1 — Histogram entropy (start here, it is the most reliable on short windows):

Metric 2 — KL divergence between consecutive windows:

Metric 3 — LZ complexity (if time allows):
